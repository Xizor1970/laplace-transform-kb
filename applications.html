Electrical Circuits

The original killer app

The Laplace Transform was adopted by electrical engineers before any other discipline because circuit elements have beautifully simple s-domain representations:

Resistor: V(s) = R · I(s) — unchanged (purely algebraic already)
Capacitor: V(s) = I(s) / (sC) — impedance is 1/(sC)
Inductor: V(s) = sL · I(s) — impedance is sL

In the s-domain, every circuit becomes a network of impedances. You can use Ohm's law, voltage dividers, and Kirchhoff's laws exactly as in DC circuits — just with complex impedances instead of resistances. The entire theory of AC circuits, transient analysis, and filter design flows from this.

Z_R = R, Z_C = 1/(sC), Z_L = sL Replace every component with its s-domain impedance, then analyse as if DC
An electrical circuit in the s-domain is like a plumbing system where every pipe has a "flow resistance" that depends on how fast the pressure oscillates. A capacitor is a flexible membrane (easy at DC, stiff at high frequency). An inductor is a heavy water wheel (resists change, easy at steady state). The Laplace variable s encodes both the DC and oscillatory behaviour simultaneously.

Example: RLC Series Circuit

For a series RLC circuit with input voltage Vin(t) and output across the capacitor:

H(s) = V_out/V_in = 1/(LCs² + RCs + 1) = ωₙ²/(s² + 2ζωₙs + ωₙ²) where ωₙ = 1/√(LC) and ζ = R/(2)·√(C/L) — natural frequency and damping ratio

This is a second-order low-pass filter. The poles are at s = −ζωₙ ± ωₙ√(ζ²−1). Three regimes:

• Overdamped (ζ > 1): Two real poles. Output rises sluggishly to final value. (Large R)
• Critically damped (ζ = 1): Repeated real pole. Fastest response without overshoot.
• Underdamped (ζ < 1): Complex poles. Output oscillates before settling. (Small R)

Mechanical Systems

The exact same mathematics in a different physical costume

Mechanical systems (spring-mass-damper) are mathematically identical to RLC circuits. The Laplace Transform treats them identically:

Mass (m): Force = m·a → F(s) = ms²·X(s) — analogous to inductor
Damper (b): Force = b·v → F(s) = bs·X(s) — analogous to resistor
Spring (k): Force = k·x → F(s) = k·X(s) — analogous to capacitor (1/C)

H(s) = X(s)/F(s) = 1/(ms² + bs + k) Displacement per unit force — the mechanical transfer function

Applications in mechanical engineering

Vehicle suspension: Design the damping ratio for comfort (ζ ≈ 0.3–0.5 for passenger cars). The transfer function from road profile to chassis motion directly gives ride quality metrics.

Vibration isolation: Mount sensitive equipment on springs/dampers tuned so the transfer function has a notch at the disturbance frequency. Used for telescope mounts, semiconductor fabrication, and MRI machines.

Earthquake engineering: Model buildings as multi-mass-spring systems. The s-domain analysis reveals which floors oscillate most (resonant modes) and where to add dampers.

The fact that electrical and mechanical systems share the same transfer functions isn't coincidence — it's because both obey linear differential equations with the same mathematical structure. A mass on a spring IS an LC circuit, mathematically. This is why analogue computers (built from op-amp circuits) could simulate mechanical systems perfectly — the electrons and the masses were solving the same equation.

Signal Processing

Filter design — shaping frequency content

Every audio filter, radio receiver, noise canceller, and equalizer is designed using the Laplace Transform (or its discrete counterpart, the Z-Transform). The goal: create a transfer function H(s) that passes desired frequencies and blocks undesired ones.

Low-pass: H(s) has poles near the imaginary axis at low frequencies, zeros or roll-off at high frequencies. Passes bass, blocks treble.
High-pass: Zero at s = 0 (blocks DC), poles at higher frequencies. Passes treble, blocks bass.
Band-pass: Poles near ±jω₀ (resonance), zeros at 0 and ∞. Passes only a band around ω₀.
Notch (band-stop): Zeros on imaginary axis at ±jω₀, poles nearby. Blocks exactly one frequency.

Classical filter families

Butterworth: Maximally flat magnitude in the passband. Poles evenly spaced on a semicircle. Good general-purpose choice.

Chebyshev Type I: Equiripple in passband, monotone in stopband. Sharper roll-off than Butterworth for the same order, but has passband ripple.

Bessel: Maximally flat group delay — preserves waveform shape. Poles cluster on a shifted circle. Best for pulse transmission.

Elliptic (Cauer): Equiripple in both passband and stopband. Sharpest possible transition band for a given order. Zeros on the imaginary axis.

All are specified entirely by their pole-zero patterns in the s-plane.

Heat Transfer and Diffusion

When the Laplace Transform meets PDEs

The heat equation ∂T/∂t = α·∂²T/∂x² is a PDE — partial derivatives in both time and space. Taking the Laplace Transform in time converts it to an ODE in x:

s·T̄(x,s) − T(x,0) = α · d²T̄/dx² The PDE in time becomes an ODE in space — solvable by standard methods

This is a second-order ODE in x with solutions involving e±√(s/α)·x. Apply boundary conditions, solve for T̄(x,s), and invert. The √s in the exponent means the transfer function is irrational — inversion requires the error function erf(x) rather than simple exponentials.

Applications: Thermal design of electronic chips, building insulation, geological temperature profiles, casting and welding thermal analysis.

Fluid Dynamics and Hydraulics

Tank levels, pipe flow, and hydraulic systems

Fluid systems with laminar flow are often linear enough for Laplace analysis:

Tank level control: dh/dt = (Q_in − Q_out)/A. With linearised valve equations, this gives first-order transfer functions. Multiple coupled tanks give higher-order systems.

Hydraulic actuators: Combine fluid compressibility (capacitance), pipe resistance, and piston inertia. The transfer function from valve position to actuator position is typically third or fourth order.

Water hammer: Sudden valve closure creates pressure waves — modelled by the wave equation, solved via Laplace Transform in time with travelling wave solutions in space.

Pharmacokinetics

Drug concentration modelling

The body's absorption, distribution, and elimination of drugs follows compartmental models — systems of first-order ODEs identical in structure to coupled RC circuits:

One-compartment model: dC/dt = −k·C (first-order elimination) → C(s) = C₀/(s+k). Half-life = ln(2)/k.

Two-compartment model: Drug distributes between blood (central) and tissue (peripheral) — two coupled ODEs giving a biexponential response with fast distribution phase and slow elimination phase.

Transfer functions from drug input to plasma concentration guide dosing regimens: how much, how often, and whether to use loading doses.

A two-compartment drug model is electrically equivalent to two RC circuits connected by a resistor — the "blood" capacitor (high flow, small volume) charges fast, then slowly equalises with the "tissue" capacitor (large volume, slow exchange). This analogy is so exact that pharmacologists literally use circuit simulators to model drug kinetics.

Economics and Finance

Continuous-time models of growth and feedback

Some economic models use continuous-time differential equations that yield to Laplace analysis:

Multiplier-accelerator models: National income Y(t) satisfies a second-order ODE coupling consumption (proportional to Y) and investment (proportional to dY/dt). Oscillatory solutions explain business cycles.

Control theory in economics: Central bank interest rate policy as a feedback controller — the "plant" is the economy, the "sensor" is inflation measurement, and the "controller" is the Taylor Rule. Laplace analysis reveals why overly aggressive policy causes oscillation.

Option pricing: The Black-Scholes PDE can be solved via Laplace Transform in time, converting it to an ODE in the asset price variable.

Why one mathematics governs so many domains

The fact that circuits, springs, fluids, thermal systems, and drug kinetics all share the same transfer functions isn't a superficial resemblance — it reflects that they all obey conservation laws (energy, mass, momentum) expressed as linear differential equations. The Laplace Transform doesn't care what's being conserved or transported; it only sees the mathematical structure. This universality is why an electrical engineer can design a controller for a chemical plant without knowing chemistry — the s-domain abstraction strips away the physics and exposes only the dynamics.

This universality holds only within the linear approximation. Each domain has its own nonlinearities (saturation, turbulence, reaction kinetics) that break the analogy. The Laplace Transform works where linearisation is valid — which, remarkably, is a large fraction of practical engineering.
