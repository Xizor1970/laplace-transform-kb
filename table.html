How to use this table

In practice, you almost never compute a Laplace Transform from the integral. Instead, you decompose your function using linearity, shifting, and differentiation properties, then look up the elementary pieces here. Going the other direction (inverse transform), you use partial fractions to break F(s) into pieces that match entries in this table.

The five essential pairs — marked with ★ — are the ones to memorise. Every other entry can be derived from them using the properties on the previous page.

These five are like the primary colours of the Laplace world. Just as every colour is a mix of red, green, and blue, every rational function F(s) decomposes into pieces that are shifts and combinations of 1/s, 1/s², 1/(s+a), ω/(s²+ω²), and s/(s²+ω²). Know these five and you can invert anything.

The pattern

Powers of t always give powers of 1/s. The factorial n! comes from repeated integration by parts (or equivalently, repeated differentiation of 1/s in the s-variable). The Gamma function Γ(α+1) generalises the factorial to non-integer powers.

The frequency-shift trick

Every damped oscillation e−at·(sin or cos) is obtained from the undamped case by replacing s with (s+a). This is the frequency-shift property in action. Memorise sin and cos, and you get all damped versions for free.

Damped oscillations are like a bell ringing after being struck — the tone (ω) stays the same but the volume (e−at) fades. In the s-domain, this appears as the same formula shifted left by 'a' — the decay rate literally moves the pole away from the imaginary axis, deeper into the stable left half-plane.

The impulse — identity of convolution

The Dirac delta δ(t) transforms to simply 1. This makes sense through the convolution theorem: convolving any signal with δ(t) returns the signal unchanged (f * δ = f), which in the s-domain becomes F(s)·1 = F(s). The impulse is the identity element under convolution, and its transform is the identity under multiplication.

Periodic Functions

ℒ{f(t)} = [1/(1 − e^(−Ts))] · ∫₀ᵀ f(t)·e^(−st) dt For f(t) periodic with period T — integrate one period, multiply by the geometric sum

The periodic shortcut

For any periodic function, you only need to compute the integral over ONE period. The factor 1/(1−e−Ts) accounts for all the infinite repetitions — it's the sum of a geometric series of delayed copies. This is enormously practical for square waves, sawtooth waves, and other periodic signals.

Memory Tricks

Patterns to remember

Denominator tells the story: (s+a) in the denominator means exponential decay at rate a. (s²+ω²) means oscillation at frequency ω. Combine them: (s+a)²+ω² means damped oscillation.

Numerator picks sin vs cos: For quadratic denominators (s²+ω²), numerator ω gives sin, numerator s gives cos. This follows from sin starting at zero (needs the ω to "turn on") while cos starts at 1 (needs the s to match).

Powers of s in denominator give powers of t: 1/s → 1, 1/s² → t, 1/s³ → t²/2, ... Each extra s in the denominator integrates once in time.

Repeated roots give t-multiplication: 1/(s+a)² → te−at. Each repeated factor brings out another power of t.

The table as a Rosetta Stone

This table isn't just a computational shortcut — it's a dictionary between two fundamentally different ways of describing reality. The time domain asks "what happens moment by moment?" The s-domain asks "what exponential modes are present?" Every physical system speaks both languages simultaneously. A spring-mass system oscillating at 5 rad/s IS a pair of poles at s = ±5j — these aren't two descriptions of the same thing, they ARE the same thing seen from two angles. The table maps between those angles.

This "two languages for one reality" view is standard in signal processing, but whether it implies something ontological about nature (that frequency-domain and time-domain are equally "real") or is merely a mathematical convenience remains a philosophical question with no consensus answer.
