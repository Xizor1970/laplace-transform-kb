Transfer Functions

The system's DNA in the s-domain

A transfer function H(s) = Y(s)/X(s) is the ratio of output to input in the s-domain, assuming zero initial conditions. It completely characterises a linear time-invariant (LTI) system — if you know H(s), you can predict the output for ANY input by computing Y(s) = H(s)·X(s) and inverting.

H(s) is always a ratio of polynomials for finite-dimensional systems:

H(s) = (bₘsᵐ + ... + b₁s + b₀) / (aₙsⁿ + ... + a₁s + a₀) Output/Input in s-domain. Poles = roots of denominator. Zeros = roots of numerator.
The transfer function is like a recipe: it tells you exactly how the system transforms any ingredient (input) into the dish (output). Give it a step input (a sudden change) — it tells you the step response. Give it a sinusoid — it tells you the gain and phase shift. Give it an impulse — it tells you the impulse response (which IS the system's behaviour, completely). One function, infinite predictions.

Poles and Zeros

The skeleton of system behaviour

Poles (roots of the denominator) determine the system's natural behaviour — what it does when left alone or given an impulse. Each pole contributes a mode:

• Real pole at s = −a → exponential e−at (decay if a > 0, growth if a < 0)
• Complex pair at s = −a ± jω → damped oscillation e−atsin(ωt+φ)
• Pole at s = 0 → constant (integrator)
• Pole on imaginary axis s = ±jω → sustained oscillation (marginally stable)

Zeros (roots of the numerator) shape the relative contribution of each mode. They determine what the system blocks or emphasises. A zero at s = −b means the system produces no steady-state output at e−bt frequency.

Stability

The fundamental question: does the output stay bounded?

A system is BIBO stable (bounded input → bounded output) if and only if ALL poles of H(s) lie in the open left half-plane (Re(s) < 0). This is the single most important result in control theory.

Stable: All poles have Re(s) < 0 → all natural modes decay → transients die out
Marginally stable: Poles on imaginary axis (Re(s) = 0) → sustained oscillation, never dies, never grows
Unstable: Any pole with Re(s) > 0 → exponential growth → system blows up

The left half-plane is like the "safe zone" on a map. Poles in this zone are like balls in valleys — disturb them and they roll back to rest. Poles on the boundary (imaginary axis) are like balls on a flat plain — push them and they roll forever. Poles in the right half-plane are like balls on hilltops — any nudge sends them careening off to infinity. Stability analysis is the art of ensuring all your poles stay in the valley.

Stability Tests

Checking stability without finding poles

For high-order systems, finding the exact pole locations is hard. These methods determine stability without solving the polynomial:

Routh-Hurwitz criterion: An algebraic test using the polynomial coefficients. Build the Routh array — if the first column has no sign changes, all poles are in the LHP. A sign change means an unstable pole.

Nyquist criterion: Plot H(jω) for ω from 0 to ∞ in the complex plane. Count encirclements of the point −1. Relates open-loop frequency response to closed-loop stability. Handles delays and non-minimum-phase systems.

Root locus: Track how poles of the closed-loop system move as a gain parameter K varies from 0 to ∞. Designed by Walter Evans (1948) — a graphical method that shows exactly how much gain you can add before instability.

Feedback and Closed-Loop Systems

Closed-loop TF = G(s) / [1 + G(s)·H(s)] Forward path G(s), feedback path H(s). The denominator 1 + GH is the characteristic equation.

Why feedback changes everything

Without feedback (open-loop), the system does whatever its natural dynamics dictate — often unstable or imprecise. Feedback lets you reshape the pole locations of the closed-loop system, effectively redesigning the system's DNA.

The characteristic equation 1 + G(s)H(s) = 0 determines the closed-loop poles. All of control design — gain tuning, compensator design, PID tuning — is about placing these closed-loop poles where you want them in the s-plane.

Feedback is like steering a car. Open-loop driving (no steering wheel) means you point the car and hope. Closed-loop (with steering) means you constantly observe the road (measure the error) and correct (apply control). The transfer function tells you how responsive the steering is — too much gain and the car oscillates, too little and it drifts off course. The art is finding the sweet spot.

PID Control

C(s) = Kp + Ki/s + Kd·s = (Kd·s² + Kp·s + Ki) / s PID controller: Proportional + Integral + Derivative — the most common controller in industry

Three terms, three jobs

Proportional (Kp): Responds to the current error. Bigger error → bigger correction. But alone, it always leaves some steady-state error (unless the plant already has an integrator).

Integral (Ki/s): Responds to accumulated past error. Eliminates steady-state error completely — keeps pushing until the error integral is zero. But adds phase lag and can cause overshoot.

Derivative (Kd·s): Responds to the rate of change of error. Predicts where the error is going and acts early. Adds damping. But amplifies noise (differentiation amplifies high frequencies).

Over 90% of industrial control loops use PID or its simpler variants (PI, PD). The Laplace Transform makes tuning straightforward: each term is visible in the frequency response, and their effects on pole placement are predictable.

Bode Plots

Frequency response made visual

A Bode plot shows the magnitude |H(jω)| and phase ∠H(jω) of the transfer function as functions of frequency ω — plotted on a log scale. They reveal:

Gain margin: How much extra gain you can add before instability (where |GH| crosses 1)
Phase margin: How much extra phase lag you can tolerate (where ∠GH crosses −180°)
Bandwidth: The frequency range where the system responds well
Roll-off: How quickly the system rejects high-frequency disturbances

Bode's insight: on a log-log scale, poles and zeros each contribute straight-line asymptotes with slopes of ±20 dB/decade. You can sketch the approximate Bode plot by hand in seconds.

A Bode plot is like an equalizer display on a stereo system — it shows you which frequencies the system amplifies and which it attenuates. Gain margin and phase margin are like safety margins on a bridge: they tell you how far you are from collapse (instability). Engineers typically demand at least 6 dB gain margin and 45° phase margin — like designing a bridge to hold 3× its expected load.

Design in the s-Plane

Shaping behaviour by placing poles

The s-plane gives you a direct map from pole location to time-domain behaviour:

• Further left = faster response (shorter time constant 1/|σ|)
• Further from real axis = more oscillatory (higher natural frequency ω)
• Angle from negative real axis = damping ratio ζ (cos θ = ζ)
• Distance from origin = natural frequency ωₙ

A good controller places the dominant closed-loop poles at a location that gives the desired speed, damping, and oscillation behaviour. Typical specs: ζ = 0.7 (5% overshoot), ωₙ chosen for required settling time (ts ≈ 4/(ζωₙ)).

The s-plane as the periodic table of dynamics

Just as chemists classify all matter by where elements sit in the periodic table, control engineers classify all LTI behaviour by where poles sit in the s-plane. Left half-plane: stable. Right: unstable. Near the origin: slow. Far from origin: fast. On the real axis: monotone. Off it: oscillatory. The entire zoo of dynamic behaviours — overdamped, underdamped, critically damped, integrating, resonant — reduces to a geometry problem in the complex plane. This is why control engineers think in terms of the s-plane rather than time-domain equations — it's the natural habitat of linear dynamics.

This "periodic table" analogy works beautifully for finite-dimensional LTI systems. For nonlinear, time-varying, or infinite-dimensional systems, no single visualisation captures all behaviour — these remain research frontiers in modern control theory.
