1. Pole-Zero Map → Step Response

What you're seeing

Left: the s-plane with a conjugate pole pair (×) and the stability boundary (imaginary axis). Right: the step response — how the system responds to a sudden input. Drag the sliders to move the poles and watch the time response change in real-time.

So what?

Poles further left → faster decay. Poles further from real axis → more oscillation. Poles crossing the imaginary axis → instability (response grows without bound). This is how control engineers design systems: place the poles where you want the behaviour.

2. The Laplace Integral — Visualised

What you're seeing

Top: the original function f(t) in blue, the kernel e−st in gold, and their product f(t)·e−st in green. The Laplace Transform is the total area under the green curve (shown as the filled region). Adjusting s changes the kernel and hence the area.

So what?

This shows what the integral "does" — it weights f(t) by a decaying exponential and sums up the result. Large σ kills the function quickly (only early values matter). Small σ lets late values contribute. The transform F(s) is this area as a function of s — each point on F(s) is one of these integrals.

3. Frequency Response Explorer

What you're seeing

The magnitude response |H(jω)| of a second-order system ωₙ²/(s²+2ζωₙs+ωₙ²). The resonant peak grows as damping decreases. At ζ = 1/√2 ≈ 0.707, the response is maximally flat (Butterworth). Below that, resonance amplifies inputs near ωₙ.

So what?

This IS what Bode plots show — how much each frequency gets amplified or attenuated by the system. An audio speaker, a suspension system, a radio receiver — all are designed by shaping this curve. The peak at low ζ is why wine glasses shatter at their resonant frequency.

4. Convolution in Action

What you're seeing

Convolution of a step input (blue) with an impulse response h(t) (gold, flipped and sliding). The green filled area shows the overlap — the integral whose value at each moment gives the output (red curve being traced). This IS what H(s)·X(s) computes algebraically.

So what?

Convolution is the fundamental operation of linear systems — input * impulse_response = output. In the time domain, it requires sliding, multiplying, and integrating (expensive). In the s-domain, it's just multiplication: Y(s) = H(s)·X(s). The Laplace Transform converts this animation into one multiplication.

Why interactive visualisation matters

The s-plane and frequency response are inherently spatial concepts — they map complex numbers to behaviours. Static diagrams in textbooks show one case; interactive animations let you build intuition for the continuous mapping between parameter space and behaviour space. Every control engineer has a "mental simulator" for what happens when you move a pole — these animations are training that simulator directly.

Visualisation aids intuition but can mislead if taken too literally. The s-plane is infinite-dimensional (every point gives a different response), and 2D animations necessarily project that richness onto a manageable slice.
